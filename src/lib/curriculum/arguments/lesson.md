# What Is an Argument?

An **argument** is a collection of sentences where some (the **premises**) are offered as support for another (the **conclusion**).

- A **premise** is a sentence offered as evidence or support.
- The **conclusion** is the sentence the premises are meant to prove.

Not every group of sentences is an argument. A passage is an argument only when some sentences are presented as reasons to believe another sentence.

---

## Worked Example: Is it an argument?

**Passage:** "It is raining heavily. The streets are flooded. Therefore, you should take an umbrella."

1. Are any sentences offered as support for another? Yes: "It is raining heavily" and "The streets are flooded" are given as reasons.
2. Is there a sentence being supported? Yes: "you should take an umbrella" is the claim they support.
3. **Verdict: this is an argument.** It has premises leading to a conclusion.

---

**Passage:** "The museum opened in 1995. It has over 300 paintings. It is located in the city center."

1. Are any sentences offered as support for another? No: each sentence states a fact about the museum independently.
2. No sentence is being presented as following from the others.
3. **Verdict: not an argument.** This is a description.

# Premises and Conclusions

An argument can have one or many premises providing support. But it always has exactly one conclusion being supported. To analyze an argument, label every sentence as either a premise or the conclusion.

---

## Worked Example: Labeling Premises and Conclusions

**Passage:** "All humans need water. Sarah is a human. So, Sarah needs water."

1. Identify the conclusion: "Sarah needs water" is the claim being argued for (signaled by "So").
2. Everything else is a premise.

| Sentence               | Role       |
| ---------------------- | ---------- |
| All humans need water. | Premise    |
| Sarah is a human.      | Premise    |
| Sarah needs water.     | Conclusion |

---

**Passage:** "The cafe must be closed, since the lights are off and no one is inside."

1. Identify the conclusion: "The cafe must be closed" is the claim being supported.
2. "The lights are off" and "no one is inside" are the reasons given.

| Sentence                 | Role       |
| ------------------------ | ---------- |
| The lights are off.      | Premise    |
| No one is inside.        | Premise    |
| The cafe must be closed. | Conclusion |

# Finding the Conclusion

The conclusion can appear anywhere in a passage: at the beginning, in the middle, or at the end. Consider three versions of the same argument:

**Conclusion last:** "Either the butler or the gardener did it. The butler didn't do it. Therefore, the gardener did it."

**Conclusion first:** "The gardener did it. After all, it was either the butler or the gardener. And the butler didn't do it."

**Conclusion in the middle:** "The butler didn't do it. Accordingly, the gardener did it, given that it was either the gardener or the butler."

In each version, "The gardener did it" is the conclusion. Its position in the passage changes, but its role does not.

To find the conclusion, ask: **which sentence is being supported by the others?**

---

## Worked Example: Extracting the Conclusion

**Passage:** "The train must be delayed. The platform is empty and the departure board shows no updates."

1. Which sentence is being supported? "The train must be delayed" is the claim.
2. Which sentences give the reasons? "The platform is empty" and "the departure board shows no updates" explain why we should believe the train is delayed.
3. **Conclusion: "The train must be delayed."**

# Standard Form

**Standard form** is a clean, numbered layout that makes the structure of an argument explicit:

1. List each premise on its own numbered line.
2. Write the conclusion last, preceded by the "therefore" symbol $\therefore$.

Here is an argument in ordinary English:

> "Since all birds have feathers and penguins are birds, it follows that penguins have feathers."

In standard form:

1. All birds have feathers.
2. Penguins are birds.
3. $\therefore$ Penguins have feathers.

The "$\therefore$" symbol means "therefore."

---

## Worked Example: Reconstructing in Standard Form

**Passage:** "You're the culprit! No one but you has had their hands in the cookie jar, and the scene of the crime is littered with cookie crumbs."

1. **Find the conclusion:** "You're the culprit" is the claim being argued for.
2. **List the premises:** "No one but you has had their hands in the cookie jar" and "The scene of the crime is littered with cookie crumbs."
3. **Write in standard form:**

| #            | Sentence                                               |
| ------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1.           | No one but you has had their hands in the cookie jar.  |
| 2.           | The scene of the crime is littered with cookie crumbs. |
| $\therefore$ | You're the culprit.                                    |

---

**Passage:** "Alex and Sarah were at the office at the time of the incident. Mark was at the warehouse on the other side of town, and there is security footage to prove it. So Emily must have been the one at the front desk."

1. **Find the conclusion:** "Emily must have been the one at the front desk."
2. **List the premises:**

| #            | Sentence                                                       |
| ------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1.           | Alex and Sarah were at the office at the time of the incident. |
| 2.           | Mark was at the warehouse on the other side of town.           |
| 3.           | There is security footage to prove it.                         |
| $\therefore$ | Emily must have been the one at the front desk.                |
