# Indicator Words

When reading an argument, the first job is to separate the conclusion from the premises. **Indicator words** are linguistic cues that tell you which role a sentence plays.

There are two families:

- **Conclusion indicators** (therefore, hence, so, thus, consequently, accordingly, it follows that) signal that the next sentence is the **conclusion**.
- **Premise indicators** (because, since, given that, for, after all, as) signal that the next sentence is a **premise**.

When you spot one of these words, you know the logical role of the sentence it introduces.

---

## Worked Example: Identifying Indicator Words

**Passage:** "Since the battery is dead, the car will not start."

1. Scan for indicator words: "Since" appears at the start.
2. Classify it: "Since" is a **premise indicator**.
3. The sentence it introduces ("the battery is dead") is a **premise**.
4. The remaining sentence ("the car will not start") is the **conclusion**.

---

**Passage:** "The exam was postponed. After all, the professor was ill."

1. Scan for indicator words: "After all" appears before the second sentence.
2. Classify it: "After all" is a **premise indicator**.
3. The sentence it introduces ("the professor was ill") is a **premise**.
4. The remaining sentence ("The exam was postponed") is the **conclusion**.

# Using Indicators to Find the Conclusion

Sometimes an argument has no obvious conclusion word like "therefore." Premise indicators can help: if you know which sentences are premises, whatever is left must be the conclusion.

**Strategy:**

1. Look for any indicator words.
2. If you find a **conclusion indicator**, the sentence it introduces is the conclusion.
3. If you find only **premise indicators**, the sentence that is _not_ introduced by an indicator is the conclusion.
4. If there are no indicators at all, ask: "Which sentence is being supported by the others?"

---

## Worked Example: Locating the Conclusion with Premise Indicators

**Passage:** "The bridge is closed to traffic, because the cables are frayed and the inspection report was negative."

1. Scan for indicators: "because" appears.
2. "Because" is a premise indicator, so "the cables are frayed" and "the inspection report was negative" are premises.
3. The remaining sentence, "The bridge is closed to traffic," has no indicator.
4. Since the other sentences are premises, this must be the **conclusion**.

---

**Passage:** "The team will win the championship. They have the best record, the strongest defense, and home-court advantage."

1. Scan for indicators: none found.
2. No indicator words are present, so use the backup strategy.
3. Ask: which sentence is being supported? "The team will win the championship" is a prediction.
4. "They have the best record," "the strongest defense," and "home-court advantage" are evidence for that prediction.
5. **Conclusion: "The team will win the championship."**

# Indicators Are Not Always Logical

Not every occurrence of "since," "so," or "because" signals an argument. These words have non-logical uses too:

| Word        | Logical use (indicator)                | Non-logical use                                                              |
| ----------- | -------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **since**   | "Since it rained, the ground is wet."  | "I have lived here since 2010." (temporal)                                   |
| **so**      | "It rained, so the ground is wet."     | "She was so tired." (intensifier)                                            |
| **because** | "The ground is wet because it rained." | "The bridge collapsed because the cables corroded." (causal explanation)\*\* |

\*\* A **causal explanation** is not an argument. It explains _what caused_ an event, not _why you should believe_ something. Compare:

- _Logical use:_ "She must be at home, because her car is in the driveway." Here "because" introduces a reason to _believe_ she is home. This is a premise indicator.
- _Causal explanation:_ "The bridge collapsed because the cables corroded." Here "because" explains _what caused_ the collapse. Nobody is trying to convince you the bridge collapsed; that is already known. The sentence explains the cause, not a reason to believe.

When you see one of these words, check whether it connects a reason to a conclusion, or whether it plays a different grammatical role.

---

## Worked Example: Logical vs. Non-Logical Uses

**Sentence:** "I have not seen her since Tuesday."

1. "Since" appears. Is it a premise indicator?
2. Check: does "Tuesday" serve as a reason to believe something? No.
3. "Since" here marks a point in time: it is temporal, not logical.
4. **Verdict: not an indicator word in this context.**

---

**Sentence:** "Since the witness lied, the testimony is unreliable."

1. "Since" appears. Is it a premise indicator?
2. Check: does "the witness lied" serve as a reason to believe "the testimony is unreliable"? Yes.
3. "Since" here introduces a reason: it is a premise indicator.
4. **Verdict: "since" is a premise indicator here.**

# Rewriting Arguments with Different Indicators

Any argument can be rewritten using different indicator words without changing its logical structure. The premises and conclusion stay the same; only the signpost changes.

For example:

- "It rained, **so** the ground is wet" can be rewritten as "The ground is wet, **because** it rained."
- "**Since** it rained, the ground is wet" can be rewritten as "It rained. **Therefore**, the ground is wet."

Swapping a conclusion indicator for a premise indicator (or vice versa) means the indicator attaches to a different sentence, but the premises and conclusion stay the same.

---

## Worked Example: Rewriting with a Different Indicator

**Original:** "The cafe is busy, because it is Saturday."

1. Identify the structure: premise = "it is Saturday"; conclusion = "The cafe is busy."
2. Replace the premise indicator "because" with a conclusion indicator like "therefore."
3. Rewritten: "It is Saturday. Therefore, the cafe is busy."
4. Check: same premise, same conclusion. Only the indicator changed.

---

**Original:** "Since the forecast calls for snow, the schools are closed."

1. Structure: premise = "the forecast calls for snow"; conclusion = "the schools are closed."
2. Replace "Since" with "so."
3. Rewritten: "The forecast calls for snow, so the schools are closed."
4. Check: same premise, same conclusion, different indicator.
